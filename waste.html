Waste and Carbon Emissions

Waste - our most underexploited resource

If I were to choose a single environmental issue that I felt has been able to successfully capture the public's attention, I would defintely choose waste management. While not without it's controversial elements, most people put their political views aside and partake in improving the management of their household (as well as non-residential) waste. People now invest time and effort to separate their waste streams, and with single-stream recycling, municipalities have facilitated the efforts of their residents. I'm sure that you've heard it used as the go-to response for those demonstrating their "concerned citizen" bona fides: "I recycle!" And we should applaud people who take this step, though it's just a first step in mitigating our environmental impacts.

However, all is not well in the realm of residential waste management, even if we achieve very high rates of waste diversion. From a carbon perspective, much more difficult decision making is required. Some considerations with respect to greenhouse gas mitigation from waste include:

  • What do we do with our existing landfills?
  • What do we do with paper waste when it are no longer suitable for recycling?
  • Which diversion programs are most appropriate food waste - composting, digestion
  • How do waste-to-energy facilites perform relative to other options?
  • How big a concern is the transport of waste to treatement facilities?

These are just a few of the questions that might come to mind when considering the climate impact of residential waste. My doctoral supervisor Chris Kennedy

and I thought it would be worthwhile investigating the Greater Toronto Area's carbon emissions from household waste, examining the entire assortment of treatment methods used in the region in 2005. This was a point in time when the City of Toronto had just started treating household organic waste in large centralized digesters, so there was 5 principal treatment approaches being used: landfilling, incineration, composting, recycling, and anaerobic digestion (AD). The total emissions we calculated from these activities is presented in the pie chart below in kilo tonnes of CO2e.

You might notice that landfills take up the bulk of 509 kt of CO2e emissions; that's because historically most waste has been treated through this means (I'll explain the historically part in a moment). Even though 40% of the GTA's waste was diverted from landfill in 2005 (this includes incinerated waste), households in the GTA still emitted a large amount - that's roughly 90 kg of CO2e per person, with just household waste. So where are all these emissions coming from? Well, lets look at each one individually:

  • Methane released from landfills due to organic matter decomposing
  • Methane and nitrous oxide released from compost facilities that aren't able to completely aerate their compost piles
  • Methane leaking out of anaerocibic digestion facilities
Nationally, non-residential waste is roughly 1.7 times (by weight) what is produced by households and 80% of that still goes to landfills (residences did better with around 67% of waste distined for the dump - Stats Can, 2012).
